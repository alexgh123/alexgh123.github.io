So, I'm back. The debate doesn't seem that articulate from my research so far, even though it doesn't seem to be a complex argument inspite of me understanding it. Generally, given my research, it looks like object oriented programming is a way to abstract a more dominant kind of programing called procedural programing. Given that computers are based on electrodes that turn on and off, I think, at their most fundamental level, computers are procedural. I bring this up, because I think it informs how computers work and how the constitutive parts will work. In other words, I agree that object oriented programming is just a way to abstract procedural concepts.

So, back to the question of how they are different. Functional programing is more in line with procedural programing, while object oriented programing takes some of the burden off the human mind by abstracting some of the annoying procedural concepts that are not as intuitive to a human.

This conversation isn't over, but the last thoughts as I work on this topic I will leave you with is this: First, generally it seems OO can support theoretical organization of information, while fucntional programming might be better for manipulating data in a more procedural way. Lastly, In my research, one comment on an article said something along the lines of, "I don't like this dichotomy because there is no reason you can't use both, they are not really really mutually exclusive".
